Project Controls — The Three Pillars

Cushman & Wakefield | Corporate Real Estate Programs

Purpose & Audience

This guide standardizes Cushman & Wakefield (C&W) Project Controls across three pillars: Scheduling & Planning, Cost & Risk Management, and Project Management for corporate real estate (CRE) — tenant improvements, capital renewals, ground‑up projects, and portfolio rollouts. Now enhanced with a specific focus on Life Sciences projects, ranging from small to medium CAPEX expansions (e.g., lab renovations, process upgrades) to large-scale greenfield or brownfield developments (e.g., biotech manufacturing facilities, R&D campuses, specialized lab complexes). Use in conjunction with client contracts, account playbooks, and C&W PMO/PCO procedures.

Tip: Establish governance early. Confirm scope/WBS, baselines, risk appetite, and reporting cadence with the client and GC/CM. In Life Sciences, consider regulatory compliance (cGMP, FDA, EMEA guidelines) and specialized equipment lead times.

Pillar 1

Objectives

Build a credible, achievable, and controllable plan integrating scope, sequence, resources, and risk.

  • Schedule basis is documented and traceable to the WBS.
  • Logic is defensible; constraints are minimal and justified.
  • Baselines and changes are governed; forecasting is reliable.
  • For Life Sciences, integrate compliance milestones (FDA audits, IQ/OQ validation windows) into the schedule.

Core Artifacts

  • WBS aligned to deliverables; coding for Area/Phase/Discipline/Vendor/Cost.
  • OBS and responsibility assignments.
  • Schedule Basis & Assumptions (see template below).
  • Level 1–4 schedules (Portfolio/Master → Control → Lookahead).
  • For Life Sciences expansions (e.g., small R&D labs), plan for pilot runs and specialized equipment setup in the WBS.
Basis & Assumptions – Minimum Contents
  • Project identifiers, baseline version, data date.
  • Scope inclusions/exclusions; landlord/third‑party interfaces.
  • Calendars (site hours, holidays, shifts), productivity assumptions & weather allowances.
  • Logistics/access (laydown, hoisting, delivery windows, building rules).
  • Procurement (lead times, submittals, long‑lead strategy, offsite prefab).
  • Regulatory (permits, AHJ reviews, inspections, commissioning). In Life Sciences, incorporate USDA/EPA guidelines for biosafety or hazardous materials where applicable.
  • Constraints/dependencies (turnovers, IT/AV readiness, client blackouts).
  • Top schedule risks & mitigations; buffer strategy.
  • Baseline change control method and approvals.
  • For greenfield or brownfield biotech plants, include extended commissioning stages often required for Good Manufacturing Practice (GMP) validation.

CPM Best Practices

  • Activities: scope‑based, measurable start/finish criteria; typical L3 durations 5–20 days.
  • Logic: prefer FS; use SS/FF sparingly; avoid SF.
  • Lags/Leads: minimize; model as explicit tasks where practical; document rationale.
  • Constraints: only if mandated (permits, landlord turnovers); avoid hard constraints masking float.
  • Calendars: per trade/site (e.g., TI night/weekend shifts).
  • Milestones: zero‑duration; flag contract & pay milestones clearly.
  • Resources: load & level to remove unrealistic peaks while protecting the critical path.
  • Baselines: set pre‑execution; govern changes formally.
  • For lab expansions, break down specialized equipment installations (e.g., fume hoods, containment systems) into discrete tasks.

Critical Path, Float, Near‑Critical

  • Critical Path: longest path driving completion; report every update.
  • Total Float: near‑critical threshold typically ≤ 10d (tune per project length).
  • Path Convergence: merge points = risk hot spots; plan mitigations there.
  • Life Sciences expansions may have multiple near-critical paths if equipment commissioning and validation trials run in parallel.

Schedule Quality (DCMA-inspired Checks)

  • Missing logic < 5% (target 0%).
  • Leads 0%; Lags ≤ 5% with justification.
  • FS dominates (> 90%); SS/FF justified; SF extremely rare.
  • Hard constraints ≤ 1%; prefer soft constraints minimally.
  • High float ≤ 5% (TF > ~44d for 1‑yr jobs; scale up/down).
  • Negative float: none in baseline; investigate in status.
  • Invalid dates: no actuals after data date; no incomplete work before it.
  • Long durations: ≤ 5% > 44d; decompose where possible.
  • BEI ≥ 0.95; Critical Path test behaves as expected.
  • Schedule stability: changes between cycles are explained.
  • In biotech pilot lines, scheduling of equipment vendors (qualification/validation) should be thoroughly checked for dependencies to avoid missing logic.

Progressing & Cadence

  • Data date tied to weekly/bi‑weekly OAC meetings; freeze by cutoff.
  • 3‑ & 6‑week lookaheads for field coordination & procurement.
  • Variance narrative: ΔStart/Finish/Float & path changes with recovery plans.
  • KPIs: SPI, milestone hit‑rate, % tasks started/finished as planned.
  • For life science expansions with clean room components, integrate stage gates for environmental qualifications (e.g., pressurization, internal finishes).

Schedule Risk (Monte Carlo)

Quantify uncertainty to produce confidence dates (P50/P80) and top drivers.

  • Inputs: clean logic, duration ranges (O/M/P), risk register.
  • Outputs: P‑dates, on‑time probability, criticality indices.
  • Actions: mitigate top drivers (tornado), resequence, add buffers, adjust crew strategy.
  • In complex greenfield biotech sites, schedule risk modeling often highlights the commissioning and qualification phases as key drivers.

Vocabulary & Acronyms

  • CPM (Critical Path Method): The method used to identify the longest sequence of tasks driving project completion.
  • WBS (Work Breakdown Structure): A hierarchical breakdown of all project work into manageable sections.
  • OBS (Organizational Breakdown Structure): A structure linking project tasks to the team or department responsible.
  • FS (Finish to Start): A relationship where a successor activity starts only after its predecessor finishes.
  • SS (Start to Start): A relationship where a successor can start once its predecessor starts.
  • FF (Finish to Finish): A relationship where a task finishes only when its predecessor finishes.
  • SF (Start to Finish): A relationship where a successor cannot finish until its predecessor starts (rarely used).
  • AHJ (Authority Having Jurisdiction): The agency or organization responsible for enforcing regulations or building codes.
Pillar 2

Objectives

Control total cost through integrated budgeting, change, forecasting, and risk that align with scope and schedule.

  • Transparent CBS aligned to WBS; clean mapping across systems.
  • Formal change/trend governance and contingency management.
  • Risk-adjusted forecasts with clear confidence levels.
  • Life Sciences projects may require additional cost tracking for validation, regulatory compliance, and specialized MEP systems (process piping, containment).

Cost Structure & Baselines

  • CBS aligned to WBS; roll‑ups by phase, discipline, vendor, location.
  • Budget types: Base, Escalation, Contingency, Management Reserve, Allowances, Owner Costs.
  • BAC: Approved baseline stored with version control; trend snapshots retained.
  • For biotech greenfield plants, consider segregating validation and commissioning costs as separate budget lines to track regulated scope accurately.

Actuals, Commitments, Accruals

  • Commitments: contracts/POs & executed COs tracked to CBS line items.
  • Actuals: invoice-based costs; maintain coding discipline.
  • Accruals: recognize received-not-invoiced to avoid period distortion.
  • In Life Sciences, you may have extended vendor payment structures for specialized lab or manufacturing equipment, leading to large accrual amounts before final acceptance testing.

Earned Value (EVM)

PV (planned), EV (earned), AC (actual). Indices and forecasts:

  • CPI=EV/AC, SPI=EV/PV.
  • EAC methods: BAC/CPI; AC+(BAC−EV); AC+(BAC−EV)/(CPI×SPI).
  • ETC: re‑estimate (bottom‑up) or formula-driven (top‑down).
  • TCPI: (BAC−EV)/(BAC−AC) or (EAC−EV)/(EAC−AC).
  • In large brownfield expansions, EVM should differentiate between base building work and specialized lab fit-out to reflect the unique cost/time drivers in each category.

Forecasting & Cash Flow

  • Bottom‑up package forecasts during execution (post‑buyout).
  • Range forecasts (P50/P80) when risk-adjusted; state confidence clearly.
  • Cash flow derived from schedule & commitment phasing; reconcile to funding cycles.
  • Life Sciences expansion cash flows often peak during equipment procurement and specialized fit-out phases; forecast carefully around these milestones.

Change & Trend Management

  • Change log: Intake → Impact analysis → CCB decision → Baseline/Forecast update.
  • Trend (exposure) log: track pressures before formal CO; min/ML/max where feasible.
  • Contingency drawdown with cause codes; report remaining contingency index.
  • In regulated environments, changes related to GMP compliance or new regulatory mandates can significantly impact cost; track these with dedicated codes.

Common Cost Reports & Metrics

  • CTC (Cost to Complete) and EAC/FAC with VAC (BAC−EAC).
  • Commitment summary: original, COs, revised, % committed vs. budget.
  • Exposure (Trends): pending potential changes; narrative basis.
  • Accruals vs. actuals reconciliation each period.
  • For biotech projects, highlight specialized process equipment spend and track it separately from general construction spend to reduce cost ambiguity.

Risk Framework

  • Identify: by WBS/CBS; include external (market/supply) and internal (design/interfaces).
  • Qualitative: Probability × Impact across cost, schedule, quality, safety.
  • Quantitative: Cost Monte Carlo with appropriate distributions (Triangular/PERT/Lognormal) and correlation.
  • Responses: Avoid/Reduce/Transfer/Accept with owners, due dates, residual risk.
  • In Life Sciences, also include regulatory and operational risks — e.g., delays in validating clean rooms or unexpected operational constraints in existing manufacturing environments.

Quantitative Risk (Monte Carlo) — Steps

  1. Model CBS by major packages; assign uncertainty distributions.
  2. Correlate linked drivers (e.g., labor rates & productivity).
  3. Simulate 1,000–10,000 iterations.
  4. Outputs: P‑curves, tornado (sensitivity), mean vs. median, JCL (cost+schedule).
  5. Decide: choose contingency (e.g., P70) and define drawdown rules.
  6. Greenfield facilities may need correlation factors for specialized MEP systems, complex building envelopes, and process equipment lead times.

Risk Reporting

  • Risk register: ID, title, cause, effect, owner, probability, impacts, response, status.
  • Heat maps for severity; highlight red/orange for leadership.
  • Tornado charts to rank drivers and inform mitigations.
  • JCL to express joint confidence of meeting cost & date targets.
Example Scales
  • Probability: <10%, 10–30%, 30–50%, 50–70%, >70%.
  • Cost impact: <0.5%, 0.5–1%, 1–3%, 3–7%, >7%.
  • Schedule impact: <5d, 5–10d, 10–20d, 20–40d, >40d (tune to cadence).
  • In Life Sciences, consider validating cost/schedule impacts of major rework or additional testing if validation fails the first pass.

Vocabulary & Acronyms

  • CBS (Cost Breakdown Structure): Categorization of project costs by phase, discipline, or location.
  • BAC (Budget at Completion): The total approved budget for the entire project.
  • EAC (Estimate at Completion): The current forecast of total project cost when completed.
  • VAC (Variance at Completion): The difference between the budget (BAC) and the forecast (EAC).
  • CCB (Change Control Board): A group that reviews proposed changes and decides whether to approve or reject them.
  • EVM (Earned Value Management): A method to measure performance by comparing planned vs. actual work and cost.
  • CTC (Cost to Complete): The projected funds needed to finish all remaining work.
  • Contingency: Funds set aside to cover identified project risks or uncertainties.
Pillar 3

Objectives

Deliver scope safely, on time, and to budget with clear governance, stakeholder engagement, and disciplined execution.

  • Define lifecycle gates and decision rights early.
  • Calibrate cadence (OAC, risk workshops, executive steering).
  • Close the loop: controls ↔ decision‑making ↔ field execution.
  • In Life Sciences, implement oversight for compliance and validation, ensuring GMP and regulatory considerations are monitored at each stage.

Phases & Governance

  • Initiation: charter, business case, success criteria.
  • Planning: execution plan, WBS/CBS, schedule baseline, risk & procurement plans, QA/QC.
  • Execution: mobilization, permits, procurement, build/fit‑out, commissioning.
  • M&C: cost/schedule/risk controls cadence; change board; inspections.
  • Closeout: substantial completion, punchlist, O&M, as‑builts, warranty starts, lessons learned.
  • For large biotech projects, insert qualification phases (IQ/OQ/PQ) as gating points before final acceptance.

Delivery Approaches

  • Design–Bid–Build (Waterfall): strong gates; late changes costly.
  • Design–Build / CM‑at‑Risk: single responsibility; overlaps reduce time but require robust design mgmt.
  • Integrated Project Delivery (IPD): shared incentives; best for complex programs.
  • Agile/Hybrid for CRE: use sprints for design packages, rolling wave planning, early enables/fast‑track.
  • For Life Sciences expansions, IPD can streamline communication among engineering, regulatory, and commissioning teams to accelerate time to market.

Roles & Responsibilities (Sample)

  • PM (A/R): governance, decisions, stakeholder alignment.
  • Project Controls (R): cost/schedule/risk integration, reporting, forecasting.
  • Planner/Scheduler (R): logic, status, quality checks, SRA prep.
  • Cost & Risk (R): EVM, trends/changes, QRAs, contingency control.
  • Client (A/C/I): approvals, scope decisions, funding cadence.
  • In a life science project, add roles: Validation/Compliance Lead (R): ensures alignment to GMP, coordinates audits.
Cadence
  • Daily: trade huddles; constraints.
  • Weekly: OAC (status schedule, lookaheads, top 5 risks, changes).
  • Bi‑weekly: risk workshop; QRA refresh if material change.
  • Monthly: steering; portfolio KPIs; contingency & reserve review.
  • Stage gates: Concept → SD → DD → CD → GMP → SC → Closeout.
  • In Life Sciences, add: FAT (Factory Acceptance Testing) → IQ → OQ → PQ → Operational readiness.

Scope, Quality & Change

  • Scope control: baseline statement; CCB for deviations with quantified impacts.
  • Quality: QA/QC plan, ITPs, inspections, commissioning scripts; track defects/rework.
  • Change: formal CRs with options analysis; maintain trend/exposure log.
  • In labs or manufacturing expansions, integrate protocols for mechanical and electrical system qualifications, and document all changes for regulatory compliance.

Procurement & Contracts

  • Prequal by safety/financials/capacity; maintain vendor matrix.
  • Contract types: Lump Sum (defined scope), GMP (shared savings), T&M (enablement/uncertainty).
  • Bid leveling: normalize exclusions/clarifications, alternates, schedule commitments.
  • For specialized life science equipment (e.g., autoclaves, bioreactors, high-purity water systems), factor in extended lead times and performance guarantees in the contract scope.

Safety, Compliance & Information

  • Enforce site safety plans and leading indicators.
  • Permit matrix and inspection timeline; authority coordination.
  • Use a CDE (e.g., Procore/PMWeb); naming conventions and version control.
  • In Life Sciences, incorporate biosafety regulations for labs (BSL-2/3 requirements), ensure hazmat handling protocols, track air quality and containment standards.

Closeout & Lessons Learned

  • SC certificates, punchlist closure, O&M, as‑builts, warranty starts.
  • Financial close: final pay apps, lien waivers, retainage release, contingency reconciliation.
  • Lessons learned across design, procurement, execution, and controls.
  • For Life Sciences, ensure post-project audits capture validation/qualification outcomes, user feedback on specialized systems, and any regulatory challenges encountered.

Vocabulary & Acronyms

  • PM (Project Manager): The individual responsible for overall project coordination and stakeholder management.
  • OAC (Owner-Architect-Contractor): Regular meetings to align on project status, issues, and next steps.
  • FAT (Factory Acceptance Testing): Tests performed at a manufacturer’s site before shipping equipment to the project location.
  • IQ/OQ/PQ (Installation/Operational/Performance Qualifications): Steps to validate critical systems in regulated environments.
  • IPD (Integrated Project Delivery): A collaborative approach where stakeholders share risks and rewards for better outcomes.
  • CCB (Change Control Board): The approving authority for any project changes that may alter scope, cost, or schedule.
  • GMP (Guaranteed Maximum Price): A contract type capping the owner’s cost exposure, with any overages absorbed by the contractor.
  • RACI (Responsible, Accountable, Consulted, Informed): A matrix clarifying roles and responsibilities in project tasks.
